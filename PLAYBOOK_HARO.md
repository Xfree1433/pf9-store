# PF9 HARO / Journalist Pitch Playbook

**What it is:** journalists send out daily queries on platforms like Help A Reporter Out (HARO, now part of Cision), Quoted, Featured.com, Qwoted, and ProfNet. Founders respond with substantive, useful expert quotes. Journalists pick the best ones and publish them — usually with a mention, link, or quote attribution.

**Why bother:** each placement yields a high-authority backlink + brand mention in a real publication. SEO-rich; trust-rich. Most placements are free (you give time, journalist gives the platform).

**Realistic effort:** 20–30 minutes per week sustained. Realistic results: 1–3 placements per quarter once you're consistent. Some quarters yield zero — the response rate is naturally low.

## Section C — Pitch template

The structure that wins HARO placements:

```
SUBJECT: {Exact journalist's query reference / publication name}

Hi {journalist first name},

{One sentence on who you are and why you can answer this.}

{Direct answer to the question — 2-4 paragraphs, ~200 words max. Specific, with numbers and examples. No marketing fluff. Stand-alone — they should be able to quote you without follow-up.}

If you want a longer perspective or screenshots/visual aids, happy to send. Also happy to make myself available for a quick call if useful.

For attribution, please use:

  Kennedy Pierce
  Founder, Plainspoken Foundry Nine (PF9)
  store.plainspokenfoundrynine.com

Best,
Kennedy
```

**Three rules that drive placement rate:**

1. **Be quotable on the first read.** Journalists are skimming dozens of responses; if your first 3 sentences aren't directly usable, you're cut.
2. **Lead with specificity.** "Mid-market MES quotes typically run $500/user/month plus a 3-6 month implementation" beats "MES platforms are expensive."
3. **Be useful even if not picked.** Your pitch should leave the journalist better-informed even if they don't quote you. Goodwill compounds.

## Section D — Pre-written pitch drafts per topic

### Pitch 1 — Per-seat vs flat-rate SaaS pricing

```
Hi {first name},

I'm Kennedy Pierce, founder of Plainspoken Foundry Nine — a flat-rate SaaS in two verticals (manufacturing and property management) that explicitly rejected per-seat pricing. Here's the honest take:

Per-seat pricing aligns vendor revenue with customer success — as the customer's team grows, vendor revenue grows. That sounds fair until you do the math at scale. MRPeasy at $49/user/month bills a 25-person shop $1,225/month. Same shop, 50 users: $2,450/month. The customer's underlying value extraction doesn't change linearly with headcount — many of those users barely log in — but the bill does.

Flat-rate works when (a) the per-seat math hurts the customer materially, (b) the vendor can ship a deliberately narrower product so total costs stay sane regardless of user count, and (c) the vendor is OK trading some upside for predictability.

It doesn't work when the product is deeply usage-correlated with each individual user's success — Slack, Notion, etc. — because the value really does scale per-user there.

At PF9, we charge $249/month flat for our 5-app manufacturing suite (unlimited users) and $119/month for the property suite (unlimited units). Crossover with per-seat alternatives is around 5-6 users / 30 units; above that, flat saves real money for the customer.

Happy to provide screenshots of our pricing comparison pages if useful.

Kennedy Pierce
Founder, Plainspoken Foundry Nine
store.plainspokenfoundrynine.com
```

### Pitch 2 — Software for small manufacturers

We charge $249/month flat for 5 apps covering those exact pains, unlimited users. Some shops pair us with a focused MRP (MRPeasy, Katana) and QuickBooks for accounting; total software spend under $400/month for the operation.

### Pitch 3 — Software for small landlords

```
Hi {first name},

I'm Kennedy Pierce — I built Plainspoken Foundry Nine (PF9), a flat-rate property management platform for landlords with 5-50 units. Here's the honest market view:

Buildium, AppFolio, and DoorLoop dominate the property management software space, all on per-unit pricing models. Buildium starts at $62/month plus per-unit fees that aren't publicly disclosed (commonly reported at ~$1.50/unit/month above 30 units). We charge $119/month flat for the property suite (LANDLORDR for management + TENANTLINK for the tenant portal), unlimited units. The price doesn't change from 5 units to 500.

The genuine tradeoff: flat-rate vendors typically ship narrower feature sets. Buildium has mature general-ledger accounting and integrated tenant screening that we don't have. The right software for a small landlord depends on whether those features are non-negotiable or whether they prefer flat pricing.

Happy to walk through the per-door math or provide our pricing comparison pages.

Founder, Plainspoken Foundry Nine
```

### Pitch 4 — No-demo SaaS funnels

```
Hi {first name},

I'm Kennedy Pierce — I built Plainspoken Foundry Nine and we deliberately rejected the "book a demo" model. Sharing concrete numbers because this is increasingly relevant for early-stage B2B SaaS.

Live demos add friction in two places. First, on the buyer's side: a 20-minute calendar block costs the prospect more than $50 of opportunity cost, killing top-of-funnel conversion from anyone not yet sales-qualified. Second, on the vendor's side: a one-founder operation can support maybe 20 demos per week, capping reachable volume by the founder's calendar.

We replaced both with a YouTube-hosted product demo (90 seconds per app, 14 apps total). The funnel is now: visitor lands on storefront → watches the video → clicks Subscribe → completes Stripe checkout. Everything is self-serve. 14-day money-back guarantee replaces the trust function the demo used to serve.

Concrete trade-offs:
- Buyers who'd need a custom demo for a complex use case never convert. That's fine for our segment (small-to-mid operators); not fine if you sell to enterprise.
- Top-of-funnel volume is unbounded by founder calendar. Conversion rate per visitor is lower than demo-qualified leads, but absolute volume can compensate.
- The video does the explaining work the demo used to do. If the product is complex enough that 90 seconds can't communicate value, the model breaks.

Works best for: simple-to-explain SaaS with public pricing. Doesn't work for: enterprise sales, deeply technical products, or anything requiring custom configuration.

Kennedy Pierce
Founder, Plainspoken Foundry Nine
store.plainspokenfoundrynine.com
```

```
This wouldn't work in 2015. It works in 2026 because (a) buyers in these segments are software-fluent now, (b) the no-code/low-code tooling makes the back-end achievable solo, and (c) AI-assisted content production lets one person ship 14 product videos in two weeks instead of two years.

The honest part: revenue isn't yet at "I-can-quit-my-day-job" levels. Bootstrapped means slow growth, deliberate trade-offs, and a long timeline. The model is durable, not fast.
```

## Section F — Don'ts

- **Don't paste the same pitch to every query.** Journalists talk; recycled pitches get flagged.
- **Don't pitch when you can't deliver.** If a journalist follows up with a phone request, you need to be available.
- **Don't oversell.** "We're disrupting the manufacturing software industry" loses placements. "Here's specific data on what mid-market MES quotes typically cost" wins them.
- **Don't fabricate.** If you don't have a metric, say so. Journalists fact-check.
- **Don't link-spam.** One link in your signature is plenty.

## Section G — Tracking placements

When a placement lands, update `memory.md`'s known-issues section with a "PR/press wins" note. Also:

1. **Save the URL** — `https://publication.com/article-with-your-quote`
2. **Note the publication's domain authority** (use any free DA tool — Moz, Ubersuggest, Ahrefs free)
3. **Add a `/press/` page on the storefront** at 3+ placements (defer until you have them; building a /press/ page with zero placements looks pathetic)
4. **Share the placement on LinkedIn and Twitter** — drives small but real referral traffic and earns goodwill from the journalist
